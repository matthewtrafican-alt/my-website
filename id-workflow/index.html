Step Two — I/O Psychology Foundation
COMPETENCY MODELING — Build the Job Standard for Any Role
Custom / Any Field
Competency models define the clusters of KSAOs (Knowledge, Skills, Abilities, Other characteristics) that distinguish superior from adequate performance in a given role. They serve as the anchor for every downstream decision: what to train, how to assess it, and how to evaluate transfer. This workflow builds or imports a competency model as the first analytical step — making the entire training design job-relevant and defensible.
🔎
O*NET Framework

The U.S. Department of Labor's occupational database provides standardized KSAOs, work activities, and knowledge domains for 1,000+ occupations. AI extracts the relevant taxonomy for any job code instantly.

Source: O*NET Content Model (Peterson et al., 1999)
⚡
Critical Incident Technique (CIT)

SME-reported examples of highly effective and ineffective job behaviors. Identifies which KSAOs actually drive performance — not just what appears in the job description.

Source: Flanagan (1954)
📋
Functional Job Analysis (FJA)

Analyzes tasks by the degree to which they involve Data, People, and Things. Establishes task difficulty levels and the skills required at each level for any discipline.

Source: Fine (1974)
📊
Position Analysis Questionnaire (PAQ)

A structured worker-oriented job analysis tool that rates 187 job elements. Quantifies the cognitive, physical, and relational demands of any role for competency model weighting.

Source: McCormick, Jeanneret & Mecham (1972)
👥
SME Panel Analysis

Subject Matter Experts rate task importance, frequency, and trainability — establishing content validity for the competency model and ensuring it reflects the real job, not the assumed job.

Source: Content Validity approach (Lawshe, 1975)
🎯
KSAO Mapping

Each task is linked to specific KSAOs: declarative or procedural Knowledge, observable Skills, underlying Abilities, and Other characteristics (e.g., personality, values, motivation).

Source: Fleishman & Quaintance (1984); Campion et al. (2011)

Person–Job Fit (Kristof-Brown, 2005): Proficiency requirements are set at the level needed for effective performance — not the highest possible level. Overtrained workers in low-complexity roles reduce engagement and increase turnover. The model calibrates required proficiency to actual job demands.

Criterion-Referenced vs. Norm-Referenced Assessment: This workflow uses criterion-referenced measurement — proficiency is judged against the job standard, not relative to peers. A score of 3/5 on a competency rated "Required: 4" is a gap regardless of how it compares to the team average. This is the I/O psychology standard for defensible, legally compliant training needs identification.

Declarative Knowledge — "Knowing That"

Facts, concepts, and principles that learners must understand. Maps to Gagné's Verbal Information capability. Assessed at Bloom's Remember → Understand levels. Sources of declarative content: O*NET knowledge domains, technical documentation, SOP theory sections.

e.g., "The learner will explain the three primary causes of [job-relevant failure mode]."
Procedural Knowledge — "Knowing How"

Step-by-step skills, rules, and problem-solving strategies. Maps to Gagné's Intellectual Skills. Assessed at Bloom's Apply → Evaluate. Sources: task inventory, CIT incidents, work samples. Requires practice + feedback for acquisition.

e.g., "Given a [job scenario], the learner will execute [task] within [standard] without error."

Design Model Flexibility
ADDIE vs. SAM — Choose the Right Model for Every Project

ADDIE — Systematic & Sequential

Dick & Carey (1978); Branson et al. (1975)

ADDIE is a linear, phase-gated model. Each phase must be substantially complete before the next begins. Strong for projects with well-defined requirements, stable job tasks, and high documentation needs — the gold standard for regulated industries and compliance training.

Best when: Requirements are clear · Scope is stable · Compliance documentation required · Smaller team · Budget is fixed

SAM — Successive Approximation Model

Michael Allen, Allen Interactions (2012)

SAM is an agile, iterative alternative to ADDIE. It replaces linear phases with rapid design cycles — producing a rough prototype early, iterating on stakeholder feedback, and catching design problems before full development. Developed specifically to address ADDIE's "late failure discovery" risk.

Best when: Requirements are ambiguous · Stakeholder alignment is needed · High-stakes or innovative program · Complex skill development
SAM Phases — Three Iterative Stages
Phase 1
Preparation
  • Background information gathering
  • Performance context review
  • Savvy Start workshop
  • SME + designer + stakeholder co-design
  • Initial design ideas generated
  • TNA & KSAO inputs (from Step 3)
Phase 2 — Iterative
Design
  • Rapid prototype #1 built
  • Stakeholder review & feedback
  • Revise → prototype #2
  • Review → design proof
  • Bloom's objectives coded each cycle
  • Gagné strategy confirmed per iteration
Phase 3 — Iterative
Development
  • Alpha build → stakeholder review
  • Beta build → user testing
  • Gold (final) → LMS deployment
  • Each cycle evaluated vs. objectives
  • Krathwohl affective goals checked
  • Evaluation framework finalized
SAM1 — Simplified (Smaller Projects)

A lightweight 3-step repeating cycle: Evaluate → Design → Develop. Small teams, single modules, quick turnaround. Retains the iterative principle without the full SAM2 infrastructure. Ideal when you have SME access but limited time for a formal Savvy Start.

Use for: Single e-learning modules, performance support tools, short job aids, refresher courses
SAM2 — Full Version (Complex Projects)

The complete three-phase model with a formal Savvy Start kick-off, multiple design iterations, and structured Alpha → Beta → Gold milestones. Cross-functional team including designers, developers, and stakeholders. Produces a design proof before full development begins — significantly reducing late-stage rework.

Use for: Multi-module programs, leadership development, new technology training, high-visibility initiatives

Use ADDIE When…
  • Job tasks are well-defined and stable
  • Compliance or regulatory documentation required
  • Budget is fixed with no scope change expected
  • Smaller, well-scoped training program
  • Team has limited availability for iterative reviews
  • Content is primarily declarative knowledge
Use SAM When…
  • Requirements are ambiguous or likely to evolve
  • Stakeholder alignment is uncertain or contested
  • High-stakes program where design errors are costly
  • Complex skill development or behavior change required
  • Innovative or first-of-kind learning experience
  • Cross-functional team is available for Savvy Start
Hybrid Approach (Recommended)
  • Use ADDIE's Analysis phase: TNA + Job Analysis + KSAO mapping
  • Conduct Savvy Start after TNA — before design begins
  • Apply SAM's iterative Design + Development cycles
  • Maintain Bloom's + Gagné coding at every iteration
  • Use ADDIE's Evaluate phase for L1–L4 measurement
  • Best of both: rigor from ADDIE, agility from SAM
The Savvy Start: SAM's signature kick-off technique. A focused, time-boxed workshop (typically half-day) bringing together instructional designers, SMEs, stakeholders, and sometimes learners to rapidly generate and react to design ideas before a single piece of content is developed. It replaces weeks of back-and-forth with a single aligned session — and it produces the raw material for the first rapid prototype.

The Evidence-Based Foundation
ID MODELS & FRAMEWORKS — What Drives Every Design Decision
🔄

ADDIE Framework

The systematic 5-phase backbone. Ensures training is needs-driven, objectives-aligned, and outcomes-evaluated. Prevents "solution-first" design by requiring a defensible analysis phase before any content is created.

AnalyzeDesign DevelopImplement Evaluate
🧠

Bloom's Taxonomy (Cognitive Domain)

Guides objective writing and assessment design. Every learning objective is coded to a cognitive level — ensuring training challenges learners at the depth required by the job, not just surface recall.

RememberUnderstand ApplyAnalyze EvaluateCreate
💡

Krathwohl's Affective Domain

Addresses attitude, motivation, and values learning — the dimension most often missing from training. Ensures programs develop not just the ability to apply a competency, but the willingness to apply it on the job.

ReceivingResponding ValuingOrganization Characterizing
⚡

Gagné's Five Learned Capabilities

Classifies what type of learning is needed so the instructional strategy matches the learning outcome. Declarative gaps need different instruction than procedural or attitudinal gaps — Gagné makes this distinction explicit and actionable.

Verbal Info (Declarative K) Intellectual Skills (Procedural K) Cognitive Strategies Motor Skills Attitudes
🔍

Training Needs Analysis (TNA)

The diagnostic step that confirms training is the right solution. Three levels ensure the gap is addressed at the right level: organizational (is training aligned to business strategy?), task (what must be performed?), and person (who has the gap?).

Org AnalysisTask Analysis Person AnalysisGap Confirmation
📋

Job Analysis & KSAOs

Decomposes the job into tasks and maps the KSAOs required: Knowledge (declarative/procedural), Skills (demonstrated proficiency), Abilities (underlying capacity), and Other characteristics (values, personality, motivation). The foundation of any competency model.

Knowledge (K)Skills (S) Abilities (A)Other (O)
